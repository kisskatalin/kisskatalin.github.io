Finding Jeans that Fit

Tools → Figma, paper and pen

Duration → 3 months

The project was made for UX Challenge.

A jeans web store that provides all the info on the products, including size guides that help you to make decisions on buying jeans.

Problem Statement

Shopping for jeans and other form-fitting apparel online could be time-consuming and frustrating.

It's hard to compare different styles and to know for sure how they will fit. It's a disappointing experience when you have to return an item that doesn't fit quite right.

Research

I wanted to learn about what customers buy on online fashion stores.

Is the hypothesis correct that the customers don't like to buy trousers online?

My research questions I was searching for participants with:

  • Do they buy clothes online?
  • Do they buy jeans online?
  • How satisfied are they while browsing the selection of jeans?

I interviewed users about which type of clothes they buy.

My in-person semi-structured interview questions:

  1. How often do you buy clothes online? (Are they experienced customers?)
  2. Do you buy clothes in stores or online more often? (Do they trust in online stores?)
  3. When you buy something online, why do you prefer that way? (What motivates them to shop?)
  4. What type of clothes do you buy online? (Do they buy jeans?)
  5. Where do you buy clothes online? (Which stores are they satisfied with?)
  6. Have you ever had a size that wasn't fitting? Did you return it? (Do they use this service?)

All the info given helped me to create a better customer experience.

Analysis

Key findings based on the user interviews:

  1. None of the participants buy jeans online

    They find it risky.

    "It's hard to guess if it fits or not."
    "I buy everything but trousers."

  2. 1 out of 5 participants shop more often online than at a store

    "The store is far away. I usually find the clothes cheaper online."
    "In terms of buying clothes, stores are better."

  3. None of the participants return their non-fitting clothes

    "I always order from the same places, so I know my sizes already. If it doesn't fit, I rather sell it."
    "If they don't fit, I give them to my friends."

Before I started designing, I visited three fashion websites to check out what logic and interface they serve when they sell jeans. By reviewing them, it's not hard to see why the users are not confident about buying jeans.

AG Jeans displays many photos on their product page which is positive. But we have to scroll down under the photos to read the details. While scrolling down, the left side of the page is fixed which is not the best way because if we read the details and click for the color of the jeans at the same time, we don't see the change unless we scroll up to the photos.

Just Jeans also uses many photos. But the product description doesn't use measures because they're hidden under a "Size Guide" link where the photos are not shown. Also, the header of the website is too height, takes up more space than it should while the product page feels crowded with many photos, texts and offers.

Mother Denim has the most professional interface of them all. They use a video of the model going around in the jeans. Also, as we scroll down, the info stays fixed which is a very good solution. However, the information could be more detailed. Especially, we have to open tabs to see the details.

Target Audience

People who are fashion lovers.

To get to know the audience better, I created a persona:

Eszter (27)
Eszter is a marketing assistant in Miskolc, Hungary. She's up-to-date with all the latest fashion trends, reads fashion magazines both on and offline. Where she lives, she doesn't find the right clothes for herself often, so she looks for them online. She prefers shopping at an actual store because that's where she can try on the pieces, but if she finds something online that she really likes, she orders it.

Sketch

Looking at what the users need and what the competitors do wrong (or even right), I sketched my solution for the high-fidelity prototype.

My solution is to display the sizes in the photo of the trousers. It gives a better understanding to the user.

High-Fidelity Wireframe

Impact Overview

I tested the high-fidelity prototype with the same users who said they don't buy trousers online.

Users welcomed the idea of detailed information on the jeans. They could complete all the tasks I asked from them, including flipping the view of the jeans to view their back. They think the interface is clear, easy to read and doesn't contain any unnecessary or unwanted text. Seeing and using my prototype, 5 out of 5 participants would buy jeans online.
